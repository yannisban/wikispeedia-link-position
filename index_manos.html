Introduction

Wikipedia is the biggest collection of human information openly available to the public. It is constantly updated with new information from the users, which results in an available online encyclopedia of an enormous size. Wikipedia has been very atractive to computer scientists and data analysts, because of the links between the article pages. From a graph perspective the links between articles form a very interesting knowledge graph, which can be a subject of interesting graph analysis research. Recently, a research direction emerged in the path of understanding which links between articles are more important i.e. which links connect semantically similar articles. Scientists tried to find these semantically important links, by examining the user navigation among those articles. In this sense, a game called Wikispeedia has been developed, which challenges users to start from a given Wikipedia article and reach another article only by clicking the links that appear in the current page. This work managed to encapsulate common sense into the network of Wikipedia, and it provided insight about semantically similar entities in the graph, as well as the tactical behavior of users to reach their destination. Although there has been a massive amount of research in such networks, a question remains unanswered:
What is the visual positions of the clicked links?
How it their position in the pahe related to the selection of the user?



Wikispeedia: A seemingly simple game

Players browse Wikipedia pages, in which links are highlighted in yellow. Each article has a number of links leading to a new article. The semantic knowledge of the word makes it possible to do hypotheses on the probability to reach the Goal article.
In this example, the player must find the article "Amazon Basin" starting from the article Constantine I. The path contains 7 links already visited (Constantine I > Great Britain > Europe > Russia > United States > Gulf of Mexico > Spain > Latin).

Wikispeedia Data

The data generated by WikiSpeedia games are multiple. On the one hand, a list of each article included in the game is made available, as well as the list of all the links that each article has with the others. On the other hand, one can access a large amount of finished paths (respectively unfinished) by the players. A path is considered finished (respectively not finished) when the player manages to reach the goal article (respectively does not manage to reach the goal article and interrupts his game). In addition, we have access to a category list (one or more) for each article; we also have access to the shortest path matrix.

Game analysis

A user facing a Wikispeedia game challenge is unlikely to reach the final goal if he randomly click links that they might seem reasonable to follow. In practise, users follow a specific strategy: In the early phase of the game, they try to reach pages with high number of links, about a general topic. This pages, called hubs, are crucial as the enormous amount of links they contain can lead the users closer to their target. After reaching those hubs, the users begin to "narrow-down" their search, in order to find the final goal article.

The graph above presents the links of the page that the users visits in every step of the game. It is understood that in the early phases of the game users visit the hubs, while in later clicks the search in narrowed down to more specific pages.

Visual Link Position Analysis

A wikipedia page has a specific structure, because it is divided in three important sections, lead, body and infobox. Every one of those sections may contain links that a user could follow. Normally, lead in on the top of the page, containing a descriptive paragraph about the current page with the corresponding links. Then, the body follows, containing multiple paragraphs and links. The body of the page contains all the information available for this page. Finally, the infobox is a small frame at the right of the page, showing conclusive information about the article. Infoboxes sometimes contain links, but there are several cases where they only contain text. In our data, approximately 1% of the links belong to the infobox section, thus we decided not to include them in our analysis.

In the graph above we present how many links are located in the lead and in the body, and how many of them are clicked. We see that the users generally prefer the links in the lead.
